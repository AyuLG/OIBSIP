Peterson in 2018

Dr. Jordan Bernt Peterson (born June 12, 1962) is a Canadian clinical psychologist, author, and Professor Emeritus at the University of Toronto. Raised in the small town of Fairview, Alberta, he grew up in a rugged frontier environment that shaped his no-nonsense approach to life. From a young age, he was drawn to big questions—studying political science, philosophy, and eventually psychology to understand why people believe what they believe.

Peterson's academic journey took him from the University of Alberta to McGill University, where he earned his Ph.D. in clinical psychology. He taught at Harvard University before returning to Canada in 1998 to join the University of Toronto. There, he became renowned for his gripping lectures on personality, mythology, and the psychology of belief—courses that were so popular they were later turned into a 13-part TV series.

In 2016, Peterson became a global figure when he posted a series of YouTube videos criticizing Bill C-16, a Canadian law he argued would compel speech and threaten free expression. This led to a massive wave of public attention, media appearances, and his wildly successful book, 12 Rules for Life: An Antidote to Chaos, which sold more than five million copies. The book's practical wisdom—"stand up straight with your shoulders back" and "clean your room"—resonated with millions seeking order in a chaotic world.

Today, Dr. Peterson's influence extends across every major media platform. His podcast consistently tops the Education charts, his YouTube channel has over 7.5 million subscribers and 650 million views, and his live tours have reached more than 850,000 people in over 300 cities worldwide. Through his online programs—including SelfAuthoring and UnderstandMyself—he has helped over 200,000 people better understand themselves and chart a path toward a meaningful life. Despite personal health battles, Peterson continues to speak, write, and teach, pushing people to take responsibility and confront the chaos of existence with courage.
